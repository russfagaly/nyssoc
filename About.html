Our History

The history of the New York State Society of the Cincinnati is intimately interwoven with the history of the United States of America.

Members of the Society were founders of the nation — in the Revolutionary War and in the framing of the Constitution. They and their descendants have gone on to play significant roles ever since: United States presidents, justices, cabinet members, generals, admirals, and ambassadors have all been New York Society members.

The foundation of the Society was built on three principles: to preserve the friendships formed in the war, to keep alive the memory of the sacrifices made by the founding generation, and — when necessary — to provide support to members or their families in need.

The Society of Friends, 1783

When the New York Society — also called the “Society of Friends” — was organized in 1783, about 453 officers qualified for membership, and 235 became Original Members. These officers ranged in age from 18 to 63 years, with an average age of 32. Most were born and lived in New York, and every rank was represented: 44% of eligible Lieutenants joined, 52% of Captains, 50% of Majors, 71% of Lieutenant Colonels, and 100% of the Major Generals who survived the war. Though primarily officers of the Continental Army, the Society also included five Navy officers and one Marine officer.

Honorary members

The Institution also provided for honorary membership for individuals “eminent for their abilities and Patriotism, whose views may be directed to the same laudable objects with those of the Cincinnati.” Since the founding, honorary members have included seven Presidents of the United States, two Secretaries of State, 17 Generals of the U.S. Army, 15 Commodores, Admirals or Captains of the U.S. Navy, and 13 Ambassadors, among many others.

The Society today

Today the Society continues to grow, focused on the three principles upon which it was founded. We maintain this Society of Friends, look for opportunities to help those in need, and — above all — work to “keep alive the memory of what they did.”

The Revolution in New York

No state saw more of the war than New York — from the loss of the city in 1776, to the turning point at Saratoga, to the final evacuation of British troops in 1783.

New York was the strategic heart of the Revolutionary War. Control of the Hudson River corridor promised to divide New England from the rest of the colonies, and so the state became a battleground for nearly the whole length of the conflict — roughly a third of the war's battles and engagements were fought on New York soil.

1776 — The war for the city

After the British evacuation of Boston, the war moved to New York. The Battle of Brooklyn in August 1776 was the largest battle of the entire war, and Washington's escape across the East River preserved the Continental Army to fight on. New York City fell that September and remained the principal British garrison in America for seven years.

1777 — Saratoga, the turning point

General John Burgoyne's campaign down the Champlain–Hudson corridor ended in his surrender at Saratoga on October 17, 1777 — the victory that brought France into the war as an American ally, and the moment commemorated in Trumbull's painting in the Capitol rotunda.

1783 — Peace, and a parting of friends

The war ended where it had begun in earnest. At Newburgh and New Windsor the Continental Army kept its final encampments; at nearby Mount Gulian, the Society of the Cincinnati was instituted in May 1783, and the New York State Society followed on June 9. On November 25 — Evacuation Day — the last British troops left New York City, and nine days later Washington took leave of his officers at Fraunces Tavern.
